## Overview

Aberrations are deviations from ideal imaging. This module develops the mathematical framework for describing and correcting optical aberrations, from third-order (Seidel) aberrations to higher-order effects. We connect ray aberrations to wavefront errors and introduce tools for wavefront analysis.

## 20.1 Third-Order (Seidel) Aberrations

The Seidel aberrations are the five primary monochromatic aberrations that arise from third-order terms in the expansion of the optical path difference. They are:

1. **Spherical Aberration**
2. **Coma**
3. **Astigmatism**
4. **Field Curvature**
5. **Distortion**

### Spherical Aberration

**Ray diagram:** Rays parallel to optical axis focus at different points depending on height. Marginal rays focus closer than paraxial rays.

**Wavefront error:**
$$W_{\text{SA}} = W_{040} \rho^4$$

where $\rho$ is the normalized pupil radius and $W_{040}$ is the coefficient.

**Transverse ray aberration:**
$$\delta y' = 4 W_{040} \frac{\rho^3}{R}$$

where $R$ is the exit pupil radius.

**Longitudinal aberration:**
$$\delta z' = \frac{8 W_{040}}{(\text{NA})^2}$$

**Sign convention:** $W_{040} > 0$ for undercorrected (marginal focus before paraxial), $W_{040} < 0$ for overcorrected.

**Correction:** Use aspheric surfaces, stop position, or multiple elements with opposite contributions.

### Coma

**Ray diagram:** Off-axis point source produces asymmetric blur (comet-shaped). Tangential and sagittal rays focus at different locations.

**Wavefront error:**
$$W_{\text{Coma}} = W_{131} H \rho^3 \cos\phi$$

where $H$ is the normalized field height and $\phi$ is the azimuthal angle.

**Tangential coma:**
$$\delta y'_t = 3 W_{131} H \frac{\rho^2}{R}$$

**Sagittal coma:**
$$\delta y'_s = W_{131} H \frac{\rho^2}{R}$$

**Coma flare length:**
$$L_{\text{coma}} = 6 W_{131} H \frac{\rho^2_{\text{max}}}{R}$$

**Abbe sine condition:** For zero coma, must satisfy:
$$n y \sin u = n' y' \sin u'$$

**Correction:** Stop position (aplanatic condition), symmetric design, aspherics.

### Astigmatism

**Ray diagram:** Off-axis point produces two line foci: tangential (meridional) and sagittal. Between them, the image is elliptical.

**Wavefront error:**
$$W_{\text{Ast}} = W_{222} H^2 \rho^2 \cos^2\phi$$

**Tangential focus distance:**
$$s'_t = s' + \frac{2 W_{222} H^2}{R^2}$$

**Sagittal focus distance:**
$$s'_s = s' - \frac{2 W_{222} H^2}{R^2}$$

**Astigmatic difference:**
$$\Delta s' = s'_t - s'_s = \frac{4 W_{222} H^2}{R^2}$$

**Circle of least confusion:** At midpoint between foci.

**Correction:** Symmetric design, stop at center of curvature, field flattening lenses.

### Field Curvature (Petzval Curvature)

**Ray diagram:** Best focus lies on a curved surface (Petzval surface) rather than a plane.

**Wavefront error:**
$$W_{\text{FC}} = W_{220} H^2 \rho^2$$

**Petzval radius:**
$$\frac{1}{R_p} = \sum_i \frac{n_i' - n_i}{n_i n_i' R_i}$$

**Field curvature:**
$$\delta z' = \frac{W_{220} H^2}{(\text{NA})^2}$$

**Correction:** Field flattener lenses, negative elements, symmetric design.

### Distortion

**Ray diagram:** Image points displaced radially from ideal positions. Barrel distortion (negative): points too close to center. Pincushion distortion (positive): points too far.

**Wavefront error:**
$$W_{\text{Dist}} = W_{311} H^3 \rho \cos\phi$$

**Transverse displacement:**
$$\delta y' = W_{311} H^3$$

**Distortion percentage:**
$$\text{Dist} = \frac{\delta y'}{y'} \times 100\%$$

**Correction:** Symmetric design, stop position, telecentricity.

### Polynomial Representation

**Complete wavefront error:**
$$W(\rho, H, \phi) = \sum_{j,m,n} W_{jmn} H^j \rho^m \cos^n\phi$$

where:
- $j$ = field dependence order
- $m$ = pupil dependence order
- $n$ = azimuthal order

**Seidel terms:**
- $W_{040}$: Spherical aberration
- $W_{131}$: Coma
- $W_{222}$: Astigmatism
- $W_{220}$: Field curvature
- $W_{311}$: Distortion

### Aberration Balancing

**Balancing spherical aberration:** Use defocus to minimize RMS wavefront error:

$$W_{\text{total}} = W_{040} \rho^4 + W_{020} \rho^2$$

**Optimal defocus:**
$$W_{020} = -W_{040}$$

**Minimum RMS:**
$$W_{\text{RMS,min}} = \frac{W_{040}}{\sqrt{5}}$$

**Balancing coma:** Cannot be balanced with defocus (odd symmetry), but can be balanced with tilt.

## 20.2 Chromatic Aberrations

### Axial Chromatic Aberration

**Longitudinal chromatic aberration:**
$$\Delta f = f(\lambda_F) - f(\lambda_C) = \frac{f}{V_d}$$

where $V_d$ is the Abbe number.

**Wavefront error:**
$$W_{\text{ACA}} = W_{020,\lambda} \rho^2$$

where $W_{020,\lambda}$ varies with wavelength.

### Lateral Chromatic Aberration

**Transverse chromatic aberration:**
$$\Delta y' = y' \left(\frac{1}{V_d}\right) \tan u'$$

**Magnification difference:**
$$\Delta M = M \left(\frac{1}{V_d}\right)$$

### Achromat Design

**Thin doublet:** Two elements with powers $\phi_1$ and $\phi_2$:

**Power:**
$$\phi = \phi_1 + \phi_2$$

**Achromatization condition:**
$$\frac{\phi_1}{V_1} + \frac{\phi_2}{V_2} = 0$$

**Solution:**
$$\phi_1 = \phi \frac{V_1}{V_1 - V_2}$$
$$\phi_2 = -\phi \frac{V_2}{V_1 - V_2}$$

**Example:** BK7 ($V_1 = 64.2$) + F2 ($V_2 = 36.4$):
- $\phi_1 = 2.31 \phi$
- $\phi_2 = -1.31 \phi$

### Apochromat Design

**Three-element system:** Corrects at three wavelengths.

**Condition:**
$$\sum_{i=1}^3 \frac{\phi_i}{V_i} = 0$$
$$\sum_{i=1}^3 \frac{\phi_i P_{g,F,i}}{V_i} = 0$$

where $P_{g,F}$ is the partial dispersion.

**Result:** Zero chromatic aberration at three wavelengths (typically F, d, C lines).

### Partial Dispersion

**Partial dispersion ratio:**
$$P_{g,F} = \frac{n_g - n_F}{n_F - n_C}$$

**Abnormal partial dispersion:** Glasses that deviate from the normal line in the $P_{g,F}$ vs. $V_d$ plot. Required for apochromats.

**Examples:** ED (extra-low dispersion) glasses, fluorite crystals.

## 20.3 Zernike Polynomials

### Orthonormal Basis

Zernike polynomials form an orthonormal basis on the unit circle:

$$\int_0^1 \int_0^{2\pi} Z_n^m(\rho,\phi) Z_{n'}^{m'*}(\rho,\phi) \rho \, d\rho \, d\phi = \delta_{nn'} \delta_{mm'}$$

### Noll Index

**Single index:** $j$ maps to $(n,m)$:

$$j = \frac{n(n+2) + m}{2} + 1$$

**Common terms:**
- $Z_1$: Piston
- $Z_2, Z_3$: Tilt ($x$, $y$)
- $Z_4$: Defocus
- $Z_5, Z_6$: Astigmatism (0°, 45°)
- $Z_7, Z_8$: Coma ($x$, $y$)
- $Z_9$: Spherical aberration

### Polynomial Forms

**Radial order $n$, azimuthal order $m$:**

$$Z_n^m(\rho,\phi) = R_n^{|m|}(\rho) \begin{cases} \cos(m\phi) & m \geq 0 \\ \sin(|m|\phi) & m < 0 \end{cases}$$

**Radial polynomials:**
$$R_n^m(\rho) = \sum_{k=0}^{(n-|m|)/2} \frac{(-1)^k (n-k)!}{k! \left(\frac{n+|m|}{2} - k\right)! \left(\frac{n-|m|}{2} - k\right)!} \rho^{n-2k}$$

**Examples:**
- $R_0^0 = 1$ (piston)
- $R_1^1 = \rho$ (tilt)
- $R_2^0 = 2\rho^2 - 1$ (defocus)
- $R_2^2 = \rho^2$ (astigmatism)
- $R_3^1 = 3\rho^3 - 2\rho$ (coma)
- $R_4^0 = 6\rho^4 - 6\rho^2 + 1$ (spherical)

### Wavefront Decomposition

**Expansion:**
$$W(\rho,\phi) = \sum_{j=1}^\infty a_j Z_j(\rho,\phi)$$

**Coefficients:**
$$a_j = \int_0^1 \int_0^{2\pi} W(\rho,\phi) Z_j(\rho,\phi) \rho \, d\rho \, d\phi$$

**RMS wavefront error:**
$$W_{\text{RMS}} = \sqrt{\sum_{j=2}^\infty a_j^2}$$

(Excluding piston, $j=1$)

### Modal Fitting

**Least-squares fit:** Given measured wavefront $W_{\text{meas}}(\rho_i,\phi_i)$:

Minimize:
$$\chi^2 = \sum_i \left[W_{\text{meas}}(\rho_i,\phi_i) - \sum_j a_j Z_j(\rho_i,\phi_i)\right]^2$$

**Solution:**
$$\mathbf{a} = (\mathbf{Z}^T \mathbf{Z})^{-1} \mathbf{Z}^T \mathbf{W}_{\text{meas}}$$

where $\mathbf{Z}$ is the matrix of Zernike values at measurement points.

## 20.4 Strehl Ratio

### Definition

**Strehl ratio:** Ratio of peak intensity in aberrated PSF to that of perfect (diffraction-limited) PSF:

$$S = \frac{I_{\text{max,aberr}}}{I_{\text{max,perfect}}}$$

### Maréchal Approximation

For small aberrations ($W_{\text{RMS}} \ll \lambda$):

$$S \approx e^{-(2\pi \sigma / \lambda)^2}$$

where $\sigma = W_{\text{RMS}}$ is the RMS wavefront error.

**Alternative form:**
$$S \approx 1 - \left(\frac{2\pi \sigma}{\lambda}\right)^2$$

### Extended Formula

For larger aberrations, include higher-order terms:

$$S \approx e^{-(2\pi \sigma / \lambda)^2} \left[1 - \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{2\pi \sigma}{\lambda}\right)^4 + \cdots\right]$$

### Rayleigh Criterion

**Rayleigh limit:** $S = 0.8$ corresponds to:
$$\sigma = \frac{\lambda}{14}$$

**Common standard:** $S > 0.8$ considered "diffraction-limited."

### Relationship to MTF

**MTF at zero frequency:**
$$\text{MTF}(0) = S$$

**Encircled energy:** Related to Strehl ratio through PSF integration.

## 20.5 Wavefront Sensing

### Shack-Hartmann Wavefront Sensor

**Principle:** Array of lenslets samples local wavefront slope.

**Lenslet array:** Pitch $p$, focal length $f_{\text{lenslet}}$.

**Local slope:**
$$\alpha_x = \frac{\Delta x}{f_{\text{lenslet}}}$$

where $\Delta x$ is the spot displacement.

**Wavefront gradient:**
$$\nabla W = \frac{\lambda}{2\pi} \frac{\Delta \mathbf{r}}{f_{\text{lenslet}}}$$

**Reconstruction:** Integrate gradients to recover wavefront:

$$W(x,y) = \int \nabla W \cdot d\mathbf{r}$$

**Methods:** Southwell, Hudgin, Fried geometries for integration.

### Curvature Sensor

**Principle:** Measure wavefront curvature from intensity differences.

**Intensity transport equation:**
$$\frac{\partial I}{\partial z} = -\frac{1}{k} \nabla \cdot (I \nabla W)$$

**Curvature measurement:** Intensity at two defocused planes:

$$\frac{I_+ - I_-}{I_+ + I_-} = \frac{\Delta z}{k} \nabla^2 W$$

where $\Delta z$ is the defocus distance.

**Reconstruction:** Solve Poisson equation:
$$\nabla^2 W = \frac{k}{\Delta z} \frac{I_+ - I_-}{I_+ + I_-}$$

### Pyramid Wavefront Sensor

**Principle:** Four-sided pyramid splits beam, measures intensity in four quadrants.

**Slope measurement:**
$$\alpha_x \propto I_1 + I_2 - I_3 - I_4$$
$$\alpha_y \propto I_1 + I_4 - I_2 - I_3$$

**Advantages:** High sensitivity, works with extended sources.

### Interferometric Wavefront Sensing

**Twyman-Green interferometer:** Measures phase difference between reference and test beams.

**Phase measurement:**
$$\phi(x,y) = \frac{4\pi}{\lambda} W(x,y)$$

**Fringe analysis:** Extract phase from interference pattern.

**Phase-shifting:** Multiple measurements with phase steps to extract phase unambiguously.

## Summary

Aberration theory provides:
- **Classification:** Five Seidel aberrations plus chromatic
- **Quantification:** Wavefront error coefficients, Zernike expansion
- **Correction:** Design strategies for each aberration type
- **Analysis:** Strehl ratio, wavefront sensing, MTF prediction

Understanding aberrations enables systematic design of high-performance optical systems.
